Gallstones form when the special digestive liquid called "bile" becomes unbalanced, much like a recipe gone wrong. If there's too much of certain ingredients, like cholesterol, other bile components, or bilirubin, the liquid can form hard "lumps" or "pebbles."

Sometimes, a growth in the liver, like a small internal "hill" (Focal Nodular Hyperplasia), can press on the bile "pipes," acting like a dam. This causes the bile to pool and sit still, allowing more "lumps" to form into large stones and blocking the pipes. Other times, an unusual "seed" or "anchor," like a calcified fish bone, can get into a main bile pipe and act as a starting point for stones to build around it.

These "pebbles" and "rocks" can cause several problems:
*   They can block the bile "pipes" entirely, causing a significant "stone burden" and making it tricky for doctors to tell if the growth is harmful or not. (PMID: 42388947)
*   A foreign object acting as a seed can lead to unexpected blockages and issues. (PMID: 42374755)
*   Even small stones can sometimes wash out of the main bile pipe on their own, especially in younger people. But this "spontaneous passage" can sometimes irritate the surrounding areas, increasing the risk of inflammation in a nearby organ (the pancreas) if doctors later try to examine the pipes with a tiny camera. (PMID: 42391236)
*   If a stone blocks the pipe leading to the pancreas, it can cause the pancreas to become inflamed (pancreatitis) and sometimes even develop fluid-filled "bubbles" (pancreatic pseudocysts). (PMID: 42375065)
*   In tiny babies, gallstones can completely block their delicate bile "pipes," leading to severe inflammation, yellow skin (jaundice), and greatly swollen "pipes," often needing special drainage or surgery to clear them. (PMID: 42394345)

Source: [PubMed ID: 42388947](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/42388947/)

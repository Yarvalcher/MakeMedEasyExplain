Imagine your brain as a dynamic, living garden.

It starts as a small, quickly expanding plot, **growing larger and more complex** through childhood and adolescence. It reaches its most vibrant, **full size in early adulthood**. Then, like any garden, it begins a natural, gradual process of subtle change and **gentle shrinking** as you age.

Within this aging garden, some areas can develop **"worn-out paths" or "patches of dry ground"** (white matter hyperintensities), particularly in later years. These make it harder for messages to travel quickly, slowing down your "perceptual speed" – how fast you can recognize details or react. (PMID: 42286380) We also have tiny, internal **"aging trackers"** (epigenetic clocks) that can signal if key areas, like your **"memory hub"** (hippocampus), are showing signs of aging faster than expected, influencing their size. (PMID: 42322545)

While there isn't one single large study that maps out the exact average size of everyone's brain throughout their entire life, scientists do examine specific changes. For instance, they've found that the brain's overall "shrinking rate" in Lewy body dementia is less than in Alzheimer's, and similar to healthy individuals (PMID: 42383163). Other studies have looked at whether "vitamin D supplements" affect brain size, finding no significant link (PMID: 40519999), or if "physical activity" directly changes the size of your memory hub or "planning and decision-making center," also without a clear connection (PMID: 40447870).
